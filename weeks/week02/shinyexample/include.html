Instructions
This applet demonstrates a one-sided test. The blue & red striped distribution is the null distribution, and the cyan and yellow distribution is the hypothesized alternative distribution (in reality we don't know what the actual parameter value is). The dashed green line marks the p̂ cutoff value that gives the specified significance level (or false alarm/type I error rate).

There are 4 possible outcomes when doing a hypothesis test:

GOOD: The null hypothesis could be true and you decide that your data is consistent/agrees with it - you fail to reject it. This rate is known as the true negative rate.

FALSE ALARM: Null hypothesis is true but you decide that your data disagrees with it and you reject it. This is also called the Type I error rate or the false positive rate.

GOOD: Alternative hypothesis is true and you reject the null hypothesis. The fraction of the alternative distribution that is cyan is called the power of the hypothesis test (also the true positive rate).

MISSED OPPORTUNITY: The alternative hypothesis is true but you decide that your data is consistent/agrees with the null hypothesis, so you fail to reject the null. This is also called the Type II error rate or the false negative rate.

- Move the sliders around to see how the rates of error change depending on where you set your significance level cutoff, your sample size, and what the actual (but, in reality, unknown!) real parameter value is (the slider called "Alternative π").
- If you press the triangles, the applet will animate that slider.
